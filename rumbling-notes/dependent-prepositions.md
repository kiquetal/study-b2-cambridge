# Dependent Prepositions

Dependent prepositions are prepositions that follow certain words (adjectives, verbs, or nouns). There is often no logical rule to explain why a specific preposition is used; they must be learned as "chunks" or collocations.

## The Strategy for Learning
Instead of learning words in isolation, learn them with their "partner" preposition.

### Common Adjective + Preposition Patterns
Many adjectives in English are followed by specific prepositions. Here are some of the most common ones for B2 level:

- **AT**: good, bad, terrible, amazing, surprised
  - *I'm bad at math.*
- **IN**: interested, successful, disappointed, involved
  - *He is interested in modern art.*
- **OF**: proud, afraid, capable, incapable, fond, aware
  - *She is proud of her achievements.*
- **WITH**: angry, fed up, pleased, satisfied
  - *I'm fed up with this rainy weather.*

## Why it's important
Using the wrong preposition can make your English sound unnatural. Memorizing them as pairs helps you develop "language intuition," where the correct version starts to sound "right" and the incorrect version sounds "clunky."

## Grammar Note: The -ing Rule
When a preposition is followed by a verb, that verb must almost always take the **-ing** form (the gerund).
- *Correct:* She is capable **of running** fast.
- *Incorrect:* She is capable **of run** fast.
