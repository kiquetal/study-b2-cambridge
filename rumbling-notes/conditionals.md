# Conditionals: Zero, First, Second, Third, and Mixed

Conditionals are used to talk about real or hypothetical situations. In B2, you need to master not only the basic four but also **mixed conditionals** and alternative structures.

## 1. Zero Conditional (General Truths)
*   **Structure:** `If + Present Simple, Present Simple`
*   **Usage:** Facts, general truths, or habits.
*   **Example:** "If you heat water to 100°C, it boils."

## 2. First Conditional (Real Possibilities)
*   **Structure:** `If + Present Simple, will + verb`
*   **Usage:** Possible future events and their likely results.
*   **Example:** "If it rains tomorrow, we will stay at home."
*   **Alternatives:** You can use `unless`, `as long as`, `provided that`.
    *   "I'll go **unless** it's too expensive." (= if it isn't too expensive)

## 3. Second Conditional (Hypothetical Present/Future)
*   **Structure:** `If + Past Simple, would + verb`
*   **Usage:** Unreal or unlikely situations in the present or future.
*   **Example:** "If I won the lottery, I would buy a house." (But I probably won't win).
*   **Note:** We often use `were` instead of `was` with "I" and "he/she/it" in formal English: "If I **were** you, I'd go."

## 4. Third Conditional (Hypothetical Past)
*   **Structure:** `If + Past Perfect, would have + past participle`
*   **Usage:** Imagining a different past and its result.
*   **Example:** "If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam." (But I didn't study and I failed).

## 5. Mixed Conditionals
Mixed conditionals combine parts of the second and third conditionals to show how a past action affects the present, or how a general state affects a past event.

### A. Past Action -> Present Result (Identifying Past to Present Mixed Conditionals)

This type of mixed conditional links a hypothetical past event (or non-event) to a present result. This is often the trickiest one for learners!

*   **Structure:** `If + Past Perfect (3rd conditional part), would/could/might + base verb (2nd conditional part)`
    *   **If-clause:** Refers to a hypothetical situation *in the past* (e.g., `If I had studied...`)
    *   **Main clause:** Refers to the hypothetical *present* consequence (e.g., `...I would know the answer now.`)

*   **Usage:** An unreal past situation and its hypothetical effect on the present. It answers the question: "If something had been different in the past, how would the present be different?"

*   **Key Tip for Identification:** Look for the **time clash** between the two clauses!
    *   The `if`-clause often implies a past time (even without explicit markers).
    *   The main clause often has present time markers like `now`, `today`, `at the moment`, or refers to a general present state.

*   **Example 1:** "If I **hadn't missed** the bus, I **would be** at work **now**."
    *   Past: Missing the bus (didn't happen, or happened differently)
    *   Present: Being at work (is not happening)

*   **Example 2:** "If she **had taken** that job offer, she **would be** living in London **today**."
    *   Past: Taking the job offer
    *   Present: Living in London

*   **Example 3:** "If he **had learned** to play the piano as a child, he **could perform** for us **tonight**."
    *   Past: Learning piano as a child
    *   Present: Ability to perform tonight

### B. Present State/Situation -> Past Result (Identifying Present to Past Mixed Conditionals)

This type of mixed conditional links a hypothetical present situation or a general ongoing state to a hypothetical past event or result.

*   **Structure:** `If + Past Simple (2nd conditional part), would/could/might have + past participle (3rd conditional part)`
    *   **If-clause:** Refers to a hypothetical or unreal situation *in the present* (e.g., `If I were rich...`)
    *   **Main clause:** Refers to the hypothetical *past* consequence (e.g., `...I would have bought that car yesterday.`)

*   **Usage:** It describes how a general characteristic, skill, or ongoing situation *now* would have affected a *past* event. It answers: "If something were different about my present state, how would a past event have been different?"

*   **Key Tip for Identification:** Look for the **time clash** again, but the other way around!
    *   The `if`-clause often describes a general present condition, a characteristic, or an ability/lack of ability *right now*.
    *   The main clause will refer to a *specific past event or result*.

*   **Example 1:** "If I **were** more careful, I **wouldn't have broken** the glass." (I am generally a clumsy person, so I broke the glass in the past).
    *   Present: Being careful (general state/characteristic)
    *   Past: Breaking the glass (specific past event)

*   **Example 2:** "If she **spoke** French, she **could have understood** what they said yesterday."
    *   Present: Speaking French (present ability)
    *   Past: Understanding what they said (specific past event)

*   **Example 3:** "If he **weren't** so busy, he **would have come** to the party last night."
    *   Present: Being busy (present state)
    *   Past: Coming to the party (specific past event)

## 6. Alternatives to "If"
*   **Unless:** If... not.
*   **Provided / Providing (that):** If and only if.
*   **As long as:** If and only if.
*   **Even if:** Whether or not.
*   **In case:** Because something might happen. (Note: "I'll take an umbrella in case it rains" vs "I'll take an umbrella if it rains").
